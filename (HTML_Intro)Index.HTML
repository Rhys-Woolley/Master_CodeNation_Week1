I became interested in Code Nation because I developed an interest in software
programming during my Level 3 BTEC in Tameside College. When leaving highschool,
you're asked the tricky question of what you want to do when you're older. Like
I'd imagine a lot of boys did, I wanted to be a game developer. At the time I
had an idea in my head of what thatmeant that turned out to be something I'd
immediately be warned about going into College. It's not what you think it is.
"Game Developer" is just a fancy phrase for "Games Programmer", meaning it's software
programming that's made for the entertainment of users, in its simplest form. While
I wouldn't quite describe it it as a lifelong dream career or anything, I'm still
interested enough in the process of software programming and game design to want to
develop my knowlegde and ability with programming languages, and Code Nation offers
at least a few courses on the most commonly used and useful ones.

My primary flaw is getting information I learn to stick. In programming, it's apparently
not uncommon and a large part of working "in the field" is knowing how to research
your issue to quickly find a solution that likely already exists. This is certainly
something I'm going to focus on in terms of problem-solving, which I consider
myself to be good at, at least in terms of commitment and motivation. In a lot of
courses that involve some kind of project towards the end, what I have in mind
ends up being reliably underwhelming. This is almost definitely because I am
famously bad at planning. I much prefer to improvise and "solve" problems as they
come, but as you would expect there's a reason that's not taught in schools or any
other educatator. I'm the kind of person who usually gets new ideas and develops as
I go along, which is fine in some circumstances, but not when working on a coding
project with a time limit. For this course's project, I want to set myself the goal
of creating something that meets all the initial specifications I come up with at the
start of the planning stage. I don't want to be in a position where I say "I won't have
time to do that, we'll strike that one off the list" or cut anything out of the project
to get it into a functional state on presentation day. I want a fully functional, complete
project with all the bells and whistles.
